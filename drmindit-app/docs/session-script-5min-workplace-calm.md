### Voice Direction Notes for Recording Engineer

```
DIRECTION: The voice should feel like a trusted, calm colleague — not a guru,
therapist, or spiritual guide. Imagine a senior professional who is highly
competent and emotionally grounded, guiding a peer through a moment of difficulty.

REGISTER: Mid-low baritone, chest voice. No whispering. No breathy quality.
No vocal fry. Maintain consistent resonance throughout.

ENERGY ARC:
  0:00–0:30  → Arrival energy. Slightly warmer, welcoming. 105 WPM.
  0:30–1:30  → Grounding phase. Slows to 95 WPM. Increased pauses.
  1:30–3:00  → Breathing phase. Slowest pace, 90 WPM. Long silences.
  3:00–4:15  → Regulation phase. Slightly warmer. 95 WPM.
  4:15–5:00  → Closing phase. Returns to 100 WPM. Confident, forward-leaning.

MICROPHONE: Maintain consistent 6-inch distance. No plosive emphasis.
BREATH: Audible natural breaths are acceptable between paragraphs but should
not be emphasised. Do NOT inhale audibly before a sentence.
```

## Part 2 — Background Audio Design

### Audio Engineering Specification

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| **Primary layer** | Binaural beats at **10 Hz** (alpha wave range) |
| **Base frequency** | Left ear: 200 Hz / Right ear: 210 Hz (10 Hz differential) |
| **Amplitude** | –30 dB relative to voice (barely perceptible consciously) |
| **Secondary layer** | Neutral ambient drone — continuous, non-harmonic, centered at 180 Hz |
| **Drone amplitude** | –36 dB relative to voice |
| **Fade-in** | 15 seconds (linear ramp from silence to target amplitude) |
| **Fade-out** | 20 seconds (begins at 4:40, linear ramp to silence by 5:00) |
| **Looping** | Seamless — no detectable loop point |
| **Melody** | None |
| **Rhythm** | None |
| **Dynamic changes** | None — constant amplitude throughout. No modulation, no frequency sweeps |
| **Sample rate** | 48 kHz / 24-bit WAV (production master) |
| **Delivery format** | AAC 256 kbps stereo (app delivery) |

### Audio Production Notes

```
MIXING:

1. Voice is always the dominant element. At no point should background audio
   compete with or distract from voice intelligibility.

2. Voice channel: Mono, center-panned. Gentle high-pass filter at 80 Hz to
   remove room rumble. Light dynamic compression (2:1 ratio, -18 dB threshold)
   to maintain consistent level without audible pumping.

3. Binaural layer: True stereo — left and right channels carry distinct
   frequencies. Do NOT sum to mono. The 10 Hz differential is only effective
   when heard through headphones with channel separation.

4. Ambient drone: Mono, center-panned. Pure additive synthesis — no harmonic
   overtones, no FM modulation. The purpose is to mask environmental noise
   (office HVAC, keyboard sounds) without drawing attention.

5. Final master: -14 LUFS integrated loudness (matches typical podcast/
   audiobook loudness for consistent playback across devices).

6. No normalization peaks above -1 dBTP (true peak).

7. During [Pause 5 seconds] markers, the binaural layer continues unchanged.
   Do NOT reduce or increase background audio during pauses.
```

### Why 10 Hz Alpha Frequency — Scientific Rationale

**Alpha oscillations (8–12 Hz)** are the brain's natural resting-state rhythm when a person is awake but relaxed with eyes closed. The 10 Hz midpoint of the alpha band was selected for the following evidence-based reasons:

1. **Cortical idle rhythm activation.** EEG research (Klimesch, 2012) demonstrates that externally entrained alpha oscillations reduce thalamo-cortical arousal, producing a state of calm alertness — awake but not hypervigilant. This is the ideal post-session state for returning to work.

2. **Stress biomarker reduction.** A controlled trial (Chaieb et al., 2015) found that 10 Hz binaural beat exposure for as little as 3 minutes produced measurable reductions in salivary cortisol and self-reported anxiety (STAI scores) compared to control conditions.

3. **No sedation risk.** Unlike lower theta (4–7 Hz) or delta (1–4 Hz) frequencies, 10 Hz alpha entrainment does not induce drowsiness. This is critical for a workplace application where users need to return to cognitive tasks immediately after the session.

4. **Safe amplitude threshold.** At –30 dB below voice, the binaural beat is perceived subliminally by most listeners. This avoids the distraction or discomfort some users report with louder binaural presentations.

**Neurological mechanism:** The auditory cortex processes the two slightly different frequencies (200 Hz L / 210 Hz R) and the superior olivary complex generates a perceived "beat" at the difference frequency (10 Hz). This entrains cortical oscillations via phase-locking — a well-documented auditory steady-state response (ASSR).

## Part 3 — Full Session Script

**Total word count:** ~520 words
**Total runtime:** 5 minutes 00 seconds (including pauses)

### Timing Key

| Marker | Duration |
|---|---|
| `[Pause 3s]` | 3 seconds of silence (voice only — background audio continues) |
| `[Pause 5s]` | 5 seconds of silence |
| `[Soft tone shift]` | Audio engineer: fade ambient drone down 3 dB over 2 seconds, then return over 2 seconds — a barely perceptible "softening" |
| `[BG: Fade in]` | Background audio begins its 15-second fade-in |
| `[BG: Fade out]` | Background audio begins its 20-second fade-out |

---

### ⏱️ 0:00 — ARRIVAL (30 seconds)

`[BG: Fade in — binaural 10 Hz + ambient drone begin over 15 seconds]`

> Wherever you are right now — at your desk, in a quiet room, between meetings — this is your time.

`[Pause 3s]`

> You don't need to change anything about your posture. You don't need to close your eyes if that doesn't feel right. Simply allow yourself to be here for the next five minutes.

`[Pause 3s]`

> Everything waiting for you will still be there in five minutes. But for now, you've chosen to pause. And that's a good decision.

`[Pause 3s]`

---

### ⏱️ 0:30 — GROUNDING PHASE (60 seconds)

> Let's start by noticing what's already happening in your body, without trying to change anything.

`[Pause 3s]`

> Notice the weight of your body in the chair. Feel the contact between your feet and the floor — or whatever surface supports you right now.

`[Pause 5s]`

> Now bring your attention to your hands. Notice where they are — on a desk, on your lap, holding a device. Feel the temperature of your fingertips. Are they warm? Cool? Just notice.

`[Pause 5s]`

`[Soft tone shift]`

> You've just done something important. You've moved your attention from the stream of thoughts to the reality of your physical body. This is called grounding, and it works because your sensory cortex and your worry circuits cannot operate at full capacity at the same time. You've chosen the sensory channel. That's regulation in action.

`[Pause 3s]`

---

### ⏱️ 1:30 — BREATHING PHASE (90 seconds)

> Now we'll use your breathing to send a direct signal to your nervous system. You don't need to breathe in any special way. We're going to use a natural rhythm — inhale through the nose, and a slightly longer exhale through the mouth.

`[Pause 3s]`

> Let's begin. Breathe in through your nose for a count of four.

`[Pause 5s]`

> And out through your mouth, slowly, for a count of six. Let the exhale be effortless. Like a sigh.

`[Pause 5s]`

> Again. In through the nose — two, three, four.

`[Pause 5s]`

> And out — two, three, four, five, six.

`[Pause 5s]`

> One more time. In — steady and smooth.

`[Pause 5s]`

> And out — releasing any tension you don't need right now.

`[Pause 5s]`

`[Soft tone shift]`

> Good. That extended exhale directly stimulates the vagus nerve — the longest nerve in your body — which signals your brain to reduce your heart rate and lower cortisol production. It's not a belief. It's physiology. And you just activated it.

`[Pause 3s]`

---

### ⏱️ 3:00 — EMOTIONAL REGULATION (75 seconds)

> Now, without analyzing or trying to fix anything, I'd like you to simply name what you're feeling in this moment. Not a story — just one or two words.

`[Pause 5s]`

> Whatever word came up — tense, tired, frustrated, uncertain, fine — it's accurate. Naming an emotion engages the prefrontal cortex and reduces the intensity of the amygdala's response. In clinical research, this is called affect labeling, and it measurably reduces emotional reactivity.

`[Pause 3s]`

> So the emotion you just named? Its intensity has already decreased, simply because you observed it and gave it a word.

`[Pause 3s]`

`[Soft tone shift]`

> Now set it down. Not because it's wrong, but because you've acknowledged it. You don't need to carry it into the next hour. You've processed the signal — the message has been received.

`[Pause 5s]`

---

### ⏱️ 4:15 — CLOSING & CONFIDENCE ANCHOR (45 seconds)

> In the last five minutes, you've done three things that clinical research supports for immediate anxiety reduction: you grounded yourself in physical sensation, you regulated your nervous system through controlled breathing, and you reduced emotional intensity through naming.

`[Pause 3s]`

> These are not abstract techniques. They are evidence-based skills. And you now have five minutes of practice with each of them.

`[Pause 3s]`

> As you return to your work — whatever is next on your calendar, your to-do list, or your inbox — carry this with you: you are someone who can pause, assess, and regulate. That is a professional strength, not a weakness.

`[Pause 3s]`

> Take one final breath at your own pace.

`[Pause 5s]`

`[BG: Fade out — 20 seconds from here to 5:00]`

> This session is complete. You're ready.

`[Pause 3s]`

`[End — total runtime: 5:00]`

## Part 4 — Psychological Rationale

### Why This Script Works — Evidence Base

| Technique | Time in Script | Mechanism | Evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Sensory grounding (5-4-3-2-1 variant)** | 0:30–1:30 | Redirects attention from default mode network (rumination) to somatosensory cortex | Ehlers & Clark (2000); used in NICE guidelines for acute anxiety |
| **Extended exhale breathing (4:6 ratio)** | 1:30–3:00 | Stimulates vagus nerve → parasympathetic activation → reduces HR & cortisol | Zaccaro et al. (2018); Gerritsen & Band (2018) |
| **Affect labeling** | 3:00–4:15 | Engages ventrolateral PFC → attenuates amygdala response | Lieberman et al. (2007); Torre & Lieberman (2018) |
| **Psychoeducation micro-doses** | Throughout | Understanding the mechanism increases self-efficacy and reduces helplessness | Bandura (1997); common CBT protocol element |
| **Confidence anchoring** | 4:15–5:00 | Reframes help-seeking as competence → reduces stigma → increases future use | Corrigan (2004); critical for government/corporate adoption |

### What This Script Deliberately Avoids

| Avoided Element | Reason |
|---|---|
| Trauma processing | Outside scope of self-help audio; requires trained clinician |
| Deep therapeutic claims | Session is regulation, not treatment — clinical responsibility |
| Medical promises | Cannot claim to cure, treat, or diagnose any condition |
| Nature imagery | Can feel incongruent in an office environment; risks breaking immersion |
| Spiritual language | Alienates secular government/corporate audiences; reduces credibility |
| Gendered language | Inclusive design for all users |
| "You should feel calm now" | Prescriptive emotional statements increase pressure; contradicts acceptance-based approaches |

